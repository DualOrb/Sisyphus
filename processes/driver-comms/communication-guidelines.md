# Courier Communication Guidelines

## Why This Matters

Couriers are independent contractors. They do not work "for" Valley Eats — they work for themselves. Every interaction must be respectful and professional. A courier you have had negative interactions with is less likely to stay an extra 30 minutes than one you have been kind to. Communication quality directly impacts operational outcomes.

## Fundamental Rules

1. **Couriers are independent contractors.** Any communication must be respectful and professional — no exceptions.
2. **Always acknowledge messages.** Even if you are too busy to resolve the issue immediately, send a quick reply: "looking into it", "will fix", or even a thumbs-up emoji. This lets the courier know their message was seen.
3. **Never speak in an accusatory way.** It makes things worse and causes more issues. Keep your cool even when a courier is being difficult.
4. **Never argue with couriers.** If a courier comes with a legitimate concern, be kind and empathetic. What may not be a big deal to you may be a big deal to them. Arguing does not lead anywhere productive.
5. **If a courier is being too difficult**, email or ticket the driver relations team (relations@valleyeats.ca) — they will handle it.
6. **Use the courier's first name** in every message.
7. **Keep messages under 160 characters when possible** (SMS-friendly). Split into two only if necessary.
8. **Never use corporate jargon or overly formal language.** Be professional but human.
9. **Never blame the driver for system issues.**
10. **Never promise specific ETAs** unless you have confirmed data.

## When to Message vs. When to Call

### Use Messaging (Text/In-App) When:
- Sending routine updates or acknowledgments
- Responding to a courier's text message
- Sending shift-start greetings or end-of-shift thank-yous
- Quick status confirmations ("Got it", "Will fix")
- Non-urgent coordination

### Use a Phone Call When:
- You are moving an order from a courier who is already driving to a restaurant — call them to let them know and tell them which restaurant to go to instead. They may not check their phone while driving and won't get the notification until they arrive.
- The courier is driving and needs real-time information — calling is better practice since we do not encourage couriers to text while driving.
- You are too busy to type — calling lets you watch the market while communicating.
- The situation is time-sensitive and a delayed text response would cause problems.

## Communication Channels

| Channel | Type | When to Use |
|---------|------|-------------|
| In-app messenger | Text | Standard courier communication, non-urgent |
| SMS push | Text (to phone number) | Urgent coverage requests — messages couriers directly via phone number |
| SNS push | App notification | Less urgent pushes — sends to the driver app only |
| Phone call | Voice | Order reroutes while driving, time-sensitive issues, when you're too busy to type |

## Shift Communication Structure

### Start of Shift
Message all your couriers to let them know you are watching their market. This:
- Opens up communication
- Makes you seem available to couriers who may feel uneasy about messaging dispatch
- Helps if a courier's app fails — they already know how to reach you
- Reminds couriers who forgot about their shift, giving them a chance to update you early

**Example:**
> "Hello, I'm your dispatcher today until --:--, message me if you have any issues!"

### During Shift
- Respond to every courier message, even minimally
- Fix issues promptly — sometimes just fixing the issue is enough
- When moving orders from a courier already en route, CALL them

### End of Shift
Message couriers to thank them for their hard work. This:
- Helps couriers feel appreciated and enjoy their job more
- Builds your relationship with couriers
- Makes future shifts smoother
- Makes a courier more likely to help you when you are struggling

**Example:**
> "Thank you for all your hard work today, have a great evening!"

## Response Rules (Cooldowns)

| Action | Minimum Wait | Max Attempts |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| Reply to driver message | 0 (immediate OK) | — |
| Follow-up (no response) | 5 minutes | 3 |
| Assignment reminder | 3 minutes | 2 |
| Unsolicited check-in | 15 minutes | 1 |

- Maximum 2 messages before waiting for a driver response. Do not monologue.
- Minimum 3 minutes between unsolicited messages to the same driver.
- Always reference the specific order (by orderIdKey) when the conversation is about an order.

## Tone Guidelines

### Good Examples
- "Hi Sarah, order a1b2c3d4 is ready at Bella Pizza. Heading there now?"
- "Thanks for the update, Mike. I'll let the customer know about the delay."
- "Got it. I'm looking into the address issue now — one sec."

### Bad Examples
- "Dear Driver, please be advised that the order is experiencing a delay..." (too formal)
- "Why haven't you picked up the order yet?" (accusatory)
- "Please proceed to the establishment to retrieve the customer's order." (robotic)

## Scenario Templates — What to Say to Couriers

These are the approved response templates for common situations. Use the courier's first name and adapt as needed.

### Scenario 1: Courier complains about moved orders

> "Hi [courier name]. That order had to be moved because [reason: will be late for order, going opposite directions, out of town order given to a driver who is going out of town, etc]. I apologize for that but I will try my best to replace it when another order comes up. Thank you!"

### Scenario 2: Courier asks why they are only getting orders with no tip

> "Hi [courier name]. I can see how that can be frustrating! However, we do not look at the tip on the order before assigning it to a driver. Sometimes customers do not tip and we cannot do anything about that. I'm sorry that this has been happening for the past few orders, but hopefully, a good tip will come in soon!"

### Scenario 3: Courier asks why they are doing another courier's re-dispatch

> "Hi [courier name]. Thank you for reaching out. The reason another courier's re-dispatch was sent to you is that the original courier is busy with another order and is unable to make it back to [restaurant] and we still need the order to be delivered. We appreciate you doing this. Thank you!"

### Scenario 4: Courier does not want to do long-distance orders

> "Hi [courier name]. We always try to accommodate our couriers but sometimes we cannot. Part of the courier's position is to be able to do all orders that come in. I apologize if that isn't something you want to do. If you have any questions about this, please contact relations@valleyeats.ca."

### Scenario 5a: Courier doesn't think delivery fee is high enough (doesn't understand calculation)

> "Hi [courier name]. I will explain how the payment works. The base rate starts at $4.50 for the first 5kms from the restaurant and then 0.61/km after that and whatever tip the customer leaves in the app, 100% goes to you. It just depends on where you are delivering to. In town orders are less and out of town orders are more. I hope this helps!"

### Scenario 5b: Courier thinks there was a pay mistake

> "Hi [courier name]. I'm sorry you feel this way! Our system calculates the delivery fee automatically. If you feel like there may be a discrepancy, please email relations@valleyeats.ca with the order number and they will look into this for you! I'm sorry for any inconvenience!"

### Scenario 6: Courier refuses to do an order because it is not enough money

> "Hi [courier name]. I'm sorry you feel this way! However, we need a courier to deliver this order as our other couriers are busy at the moment. Could you please still deliver it?"

**If they refuse:** You cannot force them to do orders. Move the order to another courier and write a ticket for relations@valleyeats.ca.

### Courier sends a status update (e.g., running late, restaurant delayed)

> "Got it, thanks [firstName]."

If the status is concerning (e.g., "I'm stuck in traffic" on a late order), check the entity timeline to see how long the issue has persisted.

### Courier sends a complaint or request

> "I understand, [firstName]. Let me look into this."

Gather full context before responding further. If the issue is within your authority, resolve directly. If not (pay disputes, deactivation concerns, policy questions), escalate to supervisor.

### Courier sends a greeting or check-in

> "Hey [firstName], how's your shift going?"

### Courier's message is unclear

> "Hi [firstName], could you clarify what you mean? Are you referring to order [orderIdKey]?"

Do not guess — wait for the response.

## Working with Courier Concerns About Orders

When a courier communicates that a specific order won't work for them (timing too tight, delivery addresses all over the place), it is always best to try to move those "issue orders" to a different courier who can manage them more efficiently. This can be frustrating since you now have to find other drivers, but in the end it helps you out and keeps couriers from getting stressed and making avoidable mistakes.

## Escalation Criteria

Escalate to the supervisor (via `request_clarification`) if:

- Driver is threatening, abusive, or using language that suggests they are unsafe to drive
- Issue involves physical safety of anyone (driver, customer, restaurant staff, public)
- 3 follow-up messages sent with no driver response (driver may be incapacitated)
- Issue requires order cancellation or reassignment and you're unsure of the right call
- Financial impact exceeds $50 (e.g., driver claiming damage, requesting compensation)
- Driver is disputing a policy and you don't have a clear answer
- Any situation where you're unsure — it is better to escalate than to guess

For any situation you don't know how to answer: reach out to your Supervisor on duty. If they don't have an answer, reach out to relations@valleyeats.ca.

## Repeated Courier Behavior Issues

If a courier is making poor choices or acting poorly and it is a repeated pattern, make a ticket for relations@valleyeats.ca and they will follow up with the courier. For less common issues, if you feel comfortable, you may address them yourself in a respectful manner. If you are not comfortable, ask a supervisor or make a ticket.
